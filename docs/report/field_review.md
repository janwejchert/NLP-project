## Field review

The ATC Readback Verifier operates at the intersection of aviation safety, spoken/written language understanding for air traffic control (ATC), and structured information extraction. In ATC, a controller issues an instruction (a clearance), the pilot repeats the safety-relevant elements back (the *read-back*), and the controller listens to confirm it is correct (the *hear-back*). This loop is the primary defence against miscommunication, yet it depends entirely on a human catching an error in real time. Our project targets the verification step of that loop for text input — extracting structured fields (callsign, altitude, heading, speed, frequency, squawk, runway, QNH) from both the instruction and the read-back, then comparing them deterministically and flagging discrepancies. Audio (ASR) is out of scope and treated as future work.

### ATC communication & read-back safety

Read-back/hear-back failure is a long-recognised aviation safety problem. SKYbrary and the ICAO information paper on read-back and hear-back distinguish three failure modes: a read-back error the controller fails to catch, an absent read-back, and a "Type II" hear-back error where the controller does not notice a mistake in their *own* clearance even though the read-back was correct [1][2]. An uncorrected error can remain undetected until a loss of separation appears on the controller's display [1].

The error is quantified in foundational safety research. NASA's Aviation Safety Reporting System (ASRS) work by Monan coined the "hear-back problem," and an associated ASRS study analysed several hundred reports (one cited 417 reports over 29 months, April 1981–July 1983), concluding that a controller *not* challenging a read-back does not guarantee it is correct — crews must explicitly query doubtful clearances [3][4]. The FAA/DOT analysis of ASRS reports by Cardosi and colleagues classifies read-back/hear-back errors, absent read-backs, and Type II errors, and reports that controllers catch only roughly two-thirds of incorrect read-backs, leaving about a third undetected [5][6]. Volpe/FAA studies of TRACON voice communications report broadly consistent figures and find that error likelihood rises with message complexity (number of items per transmission), with headings, frequency changes, and altitudes among the most error-prone items [6]. (Specific TRACON percentages come from secondary summaries and should be confirmed against the primary PDF.) Consistent contributing factors across sources include similar/confusable callsigns, expectation bias (e.g. hearing heading 280 as flight level 280), high workload, frequency congestion, non-standard phraseology, and accent/language barriers [1][3]. ICAO Annex 10, PANS-ATM (Doc 4444), and the Manual of Radiotelephony (Doc 9432) define which safety-related items must be read back — callsign, runway, level/altitude, heading, speed, transponder code, QNH — which gives a verifier its ground-truth field set [7][8]. EUROCONTROL's European Action Plan for Air Ground Communications Safety reinforces strict read-back/hear-back discipline and standard phraseology [9].

### Speech & language technology for ATC

A substantial body of speech and language work targets ATC. The ATCO2 corpus is the largest public resource: a ~4-hour gold test set with named-entity annotations and a ~5,281-hour pseudo-transcribed set, framing ATC understanding around three entity classes — callsign, command, and value — which map directly onto a read-back-verification pipeline [10][11]. ATCOSIM provides a smaller, cleaner ~10-hour benchmark of non-prompted controller speech with orthographic transcripts [12]. On modelling, transformer NER (fine-tuned BERT) is the standard baseline, with reported F1 around 0.97 for callsign, 0.82 for command, and 0.87 for value — command extraction being hardest because utterances chain multiple commands and deviate from published ICAO phraseology [10]. Self-supervised models (wav2vec 2.0/XLS-R) and weakly-supervised Whisper have been fine-tuned for ATC and released openly, with Whisper-ATC checkpoints trained on ATCO2+ATCOSIM [13][14]. Fusing air-surveillance context (ADS-B/radar callsigns near the sector) substantially improves callsign recognition, the anchor for verifying a read-back targets the right aircraft [15].

### Existing tools and the gap

Most mature and commercial ATC speech systems transcribe rather than verify. Appareo's Aviation Speech Recognition System converts ATC audio to text for cockpit displays but advertises no read-back or clearance-verification function [16]. The ATCO2 effort centres on collection, transcription, and concept extraction (callsign/command/value) — the building blocks for verification, but not a fielded verifier [10][11]. MITRE/MIT prototypes likewise focus on transcription plus information extraction for conformance monitoring [17]. Dedicated read-back-error-detection (RED) prototypes do exist, almost entirely from European SESAR-funded research: Helmke and colleagues in the HAAWAII project explicitly perform verification — audio → text → concept, then comparing controller-instruction concepts against pilot read-back concepts using rule-based and data-driven methods — reporting roughly 80–81% detection on real recordings, with detection at the semantic "concept" level proving more robust than word-level matching [18][19]. The gap is therefore clear: verification systems remain largely at the lab/prototype stage with non-trivial false-alarm rates, and no widely deployed tool performs full automated pilot-read-back-vs-controller-instruction verification, even though ICAO doctrine requires the check but leaves it a manual human task [2][6]. Our contribution is a lightweight, auditable, text-based field-by-field verifier with an explicit error taxonomy, positioned squarely in this under-served niche.

### How our approach fits

Our design mirrors the HAAWAII concept-comparison insight while staying transparent: an LLM performs the extraction step — turning free-text instructions and read-backs into the same structured fields used as ICAO mandatory read-back items [7][18] — and every correctness judgement is made by deterministic Python, not by the model. This keeps the verification logic auditable and reproducible, separating fallible language understanding from the comparison that actually flags discrepancies, and scopes us to text so the contribution is a focused verification layer rather than another transcription front-end.
